Understanding Users

I developed the following personas based on a Contextual inquiry done with one of our costumers, and the information gathered from marketing.

PPR has two in-stores solutions: one made to print out labels/price signs in bulks mainly for store managers, sometimes by retail associates (web-app solution) and another one to correct or create tag prices on the go (usually very small batches of signs created with a zebra scanner-phone size device). This solution also has a CMS that only was visible by system administrators.

For this portfolio, I am primarily focusing on the in-store web-app solution.

Taylor - System admin (Corporate)

  • System admin, manage user accounts, decides what information goes in the signs, builds templates.
  • Needs to print out labels in bulks with pre-made templates, to be sent to stores.

Meghan - Store Manager

Needs to print out labels in bulks with pre-made templates, so that retail associates can replace the old price labels with the new ones.

Alex - Retail Associate

Needs to verify product details, and create small batches of signs/price labels on the go.

NOTE:

  • Not all companies had all 3 persona types, this varies depending on how they operate their businesses.
  • The content of this portfolio piece focus mainly on the Store Manager and the Retail Associate persona types.

The Design Space

Some of the customers at the time I was working with this product included: JCPenney, Love's (travel stop - convenience stores), BJ's, Bed Bath and Beyond, and various European supermarkets.

Retail stores have thousands of products and there are many variables associated with each item.

Requirements were gathered and channeled by marketing.

My Involvement

For this particular project we user tested another’s designer work with both real customers (remotely) and paid participants with relevant experience. We recruited participants through an agency and brought them to Lexmark’s usability lab. My job was to provide the participant screener guidliness for the agency, design the user testing protocol, help running the usability testing, and address issues found during the usability testing. PPR is a revamp of AccessVia, which was acquired by Lexmark. During my time at Lexmark, PPR was the most profitable software in the company’s portfolio services.

User Testing Findings

Some of the main issues found were:

Batches of signs were created for users and these batches were named automatically. Users were confused and could not find the batch they just had created and most of them did not even notice they created a batch to begin with, neither did they read the banner.

1

Users had a hard time trying to find the product library. Users got stuck between the item library and the the “view batch.” The pages were alike, and it was hard for participants to know where they were. Some corporate participants suggested that the word create used in the item library implied something completely new.

2

Most corporate users found the print IU had redundant information, that It was not clear what had been printed. Mainly because there was an animation pushing up an ongoing job, and replacing it with a new, therefore the print status was completely out of view. Users did not notice or understand how to select or deselect a sign from a group to print. Users did not know what the grey bubbles meant.

3

Addressing the Usability Issues

NOTE: The designs I delivered did not have the corporate colors. After my work was done, they were turned to graphic design, and they rendered the designs with the brand colors and styles.

Where is my batch?

The task given to users was to create a group of signs. Information of several products were given to participants to complete the task.

Solutions for Issues # 1 and 2

Participants did not click on the “view” button. Banner was ignored by them. “View” button would have taken users to the batch list. Instead, they went back and took other routes. When they finally landed on the batch list they thought they were in the creating batches workflow, the main reason is because the two pages were similar. All this, added to a potential customer request that the items of the item library (library of products) would have images let to the following design:

  • Visual representation of the template used to add signs to the batch with the ability to change it as needed.


  • A toggle to switch between a data grid view of the products and the image product view.


  • A sign counter that visually represents the growth of the batch. This was inspired in by the shopping cart experience we all are accustomed to.

This solution for the most part had positive feedback after testing. This solution is the final after a few modifications done after the second test.

Wait... What?

  • As mentioned before, corporate users found the duplicity of information unnecessary and confusing.


  • Users did not know what the grey bubbles meant.


  • There was an animation pushing up an ongoing job, and replacing it with a new, therefore the print status was completely out of view.


  • Users did not notice or understand how to select or deselect a sign from a group to print.

Solutions for Issue # 3



  • Real state gained by moving the print IU from a modal to a full page view that allowed to have a table view, so the information needed by users will not disappear.

  • Print status is always visible.

  • The information of the signs and pages were presented clearly to the users.

  • Unnecessary information was deleted.

  • Layout of selection was changed.
